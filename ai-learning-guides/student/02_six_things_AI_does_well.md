# Six Things AI Does Well

This excerpt summarizes Chapter 2 of the Student Guide. The full text is in
[`LearningWithAI_StudentGuide_v1.pdf`](LearningWithAI_StudentGuide_v1.pdf).

## Productive uses of AI for learning mathematics

The Guide identifies six categories of productive AI use. All of them are
examples of "the something else" from the maxim — interactions that turn
AI into a study aid rather than a completion engine.

### 1. Clarifying notation and definitions

Mathematics texts often use compact notation that elides assumptions.
Asking an AI "What does $\alpha$ mean in this context?" or "Why does this
proof use $\partial$ rather than $d$?" is a faster path to clarity than
re-reading the original passage. The AI's response is a starting point;
verifying it against a textbook definition is part of the workflow.

### 2. Generating alternative explanations

When one explanation does not click, ask for another. Different framings
of the same idea reach different students. The AI can produce explanations
in terms of geometry, algebra, physical analogy, or computational example
on demand. The skill is choosing which framing to ask for.

### 3. Suggesting counterexamples and edge cases

"Give me a function that is continuous but not differentiable" or "Construct
a model where $\mathcal{R}_0 < 1$ but the disease still persists" are
high-leverage prompts. Counterexamples are how a learner internalizes the
boundary of a definition or theorem.

### 4. Computational prototyping

"Write me a quick simulation of the SIR model so I can see what trajectories
look like at different parameter values" is a productive use even when the
student cannot yet write the code unaided. The understanding comes from
running the code and varying the parameters, not from typing every line.
This is where AI Lab notebooks (in the companion-repo `exercises/ai-lab/`)
fit naturally.

### 5. Identifying what to study

"I'm confused about Chapter 6 fitting workflow. What concepts should I master
before reading it?" is a productive prompt because the AI has good background
knowledge of mathematical prerequisites and can produce a useful study list.
The student must then study those things; the prompt only identifies them.

### 6. Self-testing through retrieval practice

"Make me a five-question quiz on Chapter 4" is a productive prompt because
retrieval practice is one of the most effective study methods known. The AI
can generate the quiz; the student does the recall.

## What the six have in common

All six involve the AI producing something the student then *acts on* —
verifying, comparing, exploring, recalling. The student's brain does the
learning work; the AI accelerates the process by providing materials
quickly. None of the six involve the student submitting AI output as their
own work. The submission test (would I submit this?) is a clean filter:
if yes, the use is probably the wrong kind.

## Connection to the book

The book's AI Audit boxes (Chapters 2A, 2B, 4, 6, 6B, 7) are the technical
operationalization of the same logic for epidemic-modeling specifically.
The audits give the student practice at the verification step that turns
the productive uses into reliable knowledge.
